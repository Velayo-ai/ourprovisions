# EVIDENCE — The financial case for the list

**Purpose:** The sourced spine for two surfaces — the `ourprovisions.app`
boardroom hero (Movement I of the sine wave) and the investor Vision Roadmap
(which currently carries **no external citations** — only illustrative in-app
figures like "$847 saved"). Every number here is real and attributed. Each
carries a **dual reading**: the *boardroom* register (market size / mechanism /
monetizable leak) and the *shopper* register (empathy / validation / relief).
Lead with the wallet, land on the life.

**Discipline note:** the clean, defensible list-savings figure is a **dollar
reduction per trip (~$10–13)**, *not* a percentage. "~10%" is derived honestly
from two sourced figures (dollar reduction ÷ average trip), never asserted bare.
The remembered "10–20%" is **not** supported for the list *alone* — that upper
range came from bundled tactics (list + store-brands + sales). Keep them
separate or a sharp investor catches the conflation.

## The spine (use these)

### 1. The list causally reduces spend — the peer-reviewed anchor
- **Figure:** List-makers spent **~$10–13 less per trip** in randomized studies;
  replicates field work going back to 1993 (~$13 in-store).
- **Source:** Davydenko & Peetz (2020), *Journal of Consumer Behaviour*,
  "Shopping less with shopping lists," 19:240–251. doi.org/10.1002/cb.1812.
- **Why it's the anchor:** *randomized assignment* → causal, not correlational.
  Survives a skeptical investor. This is the one that does the heavy lifting.
- **Boardroom reading:** a proven, quantified lever — and it's the *cheapest*
  one in retail (a list costs nothing).
- **Shopper reading:** "the fix isn't willpower or coupons — it's a list, and
  it's been in your drawer the whole time."

### 2. The leak the list plugs — impulse spend
- **Figure:** Impulse buying is **up to 62% of grocery sales revenue** (up to 80%
  in some categories); groceries are the **2nd most common impulse category (50%
  of shoppers)**; avg consumer spends **~$254/mo** on impulse buys.
- **Source:** Capital One Shopping research, *Impulse Buying Statistics 2026*
  (aggregates industry data).
- **Boardroom reading:** the size of the inefficiency OurProvisions attacks —
  and, at Phase 3–4, monetizes.
- **Shopper reading:** *validation of the anxiety* — "you overspend because the
  store is engineered for it, not because you're careless." This is the empathy
  beat of the trough.

### 3. The trip you're reducing — average basket
- **Figure:** Average grocery trip **~$108** (primary) — Progressive Grocer 75th
  CES, 1,001 US shoppers, 2024. A second 2024 survey (Drive Research) puts it
  **~$174**; the gap is method/sample. **Cite $108** as primary (larger, more
  rigorous sample); name the range, don't blend.
- **Why it matters:** it's the denominator that turns $10–13 into "~10%." Also
  proves the behavior is mainstream: **63% already make a list.**
- **Boardroom reading:** big, recurring, controllable line item.
- **Shopper reading:** "~$108 a trip, a couple times a week — of course it feels
  out of control."

### 4. Adoption — upgrade a habit, don't create one
- **Figure:** Only **~10% of shoppers don't use a list** (→ ~90% already do).
- **Source:** National Consumer Panel member survey, 2024–25.
- **Boardroom reading:** near-zero behavior-change risk — the wedge is a habit
  90% already have; we make it *smart*, not *new*.
- **Shopper reading:** "you already do this — we just make the thing you already
  do actually work."

### 5. Multi-store reality — validates Phase 3
- **Figure:** Americans shop **~2 stores/week**, **~2 trips/week**; **price
  (72%)** is the #1 store-choice factor.
- **Source:** Drive Research 2024 (n≈1,000); LiveNOW/FOX coverage.
- **Boardroom reading:** cross-store price comparison (Phase 3) addresses a
  behavior people *already* perform manually and badly.
- **Shopper reading:** "you already run around to two stores chasing prices —
  imagine the list did that math for you."

### 6. The selective shopper — feasibility + moat
- **Figure:** Of **35,372** products on shelves, the average shopper buys just
  **~260 distinct items in a year**.
- **Source:** Catalina Marketing, "selective shopper" study.
- **Note:** this is *variety* (distinct items/yr), **not** items-per-trip. Label
  it correctly.
- **Boardroom reading:** personalization is *tractable* — we don't model 35k
  SKUs, we learn each household's ~260. And once learned, switching means
  re-teaching → warm lock-in.
- **Shopper reading:** "the list learns your handful of regulars fast, then gets
  out of your way."

## Deliberately NOT used (and why)

| Claim | Why cut |
|---|---|
| "Listers save 10–20%" (bare %) | Upper range unsupported for list *alone*; conflates bundled tactics. Use ~$10–13/trip (≈10% on a $108 trip) instead. |
| Store-brand "save 25–30%" | Real (Consumer Reports) but a *different lever* than the list. Don't let it masquerade as list savings. |
| Viral "6-to-1 saves $15–40/trip" | TikTok self-report, not controlled. Good *color* ("the market is already reaching for this"), never a load-bearing number. |
| "Average X items per trip" (single number) | No clean source; it's bimodal (~14 quick-trip vs ~35 stock-up). Use the *range* or trip *frequency*, never a flat average. |
| NYT "lists save 10%" | Could not locate this session, in Dan's docs or on the web. The JCB study is a stronger citation than a newspaper would be anyway. Do not cite a ghost. |

## How it maps to the sine wave

- **Boardroom peak (Movement I):** #3 (big controllable line) + #2 (62% impulse
  leak) + #1 (causal $10–13 lever). Credible, cited, no fragile claim.
- **Trough:** #2 shopper-reading (the store is engineered against you) + #3
  shopper-reading (~$108 × 2/wk, of course it's out of control).
- **Ah-ha:** #1 (the humble list is the proven fix) + #4 (90% already do it —
  you're one small upgrade away).
- **Vision overshoot:** #5 + #6 → the list that learns becomes the gateway
  (cross-store savings, less waste, less load) — money becomes *life*.
